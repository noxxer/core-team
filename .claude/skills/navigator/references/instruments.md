# Instruments for Abstraction Level Navigation

Detailed step-by-step algorithms for all 10 instruments referenced by the Navigator skill.

Scientific references: see `references/traps.md` for CLT, metacognition, and trap mechanisms.

## Scientific Foundation (brief)

**Metacognition** (T6.1): Training reduces bias effects by 29%. Most validated meta-skill. But suppressed by stress (paradox P7) — external triggers matter more than self-analysis.

**CLT** (T6.3, Trope & Liberman): Psychological distance governs thinking level. Far → abstract ("why?"), close → concrete ("how?"). Wisdom = commanding both levels simultaneously (2024 study, N=1151).

**Concrete** reduces procrastination (McCrea 2008). **Abstract** improves pattern recognition (Forster 2004).

## Ascend Instruments (from concrete to abstract)

### Instrument 1: Three "Why?"

Ascend the ladder of abstraction. Each "why?" increases psychological distance (CLT).

**Algorithm:**
1. "Why are we doing [current action]?"
2. "Why is [answer to question 1] needed?"
3. "Why does this matter for the *book*?"

**When to apply:** goal displacement, lost strategy, stuck in details.

**CLT technique to amplify:** "If this were someone else's task a year from now?" — increases distance, weakens Einstellung.

**Best used by:** whoever owns strategic vision / alignment.

### Instrument 2: Iceberg Model (4 levels deep)

Systems thinking: from symptom to root cause.

**Algorithm:**
1. **Event** — what happened on the plot surface?
2. **Pattern** — does this repeat? Same conflicts, same resolutions, same moves
3. **Structure** — what mechanism generates the pattern? Arc, motivation, world rule, author habit
4. **Mental Model** — what *team assumption* makes the structure "normal"?

Most problems are solved at levels 1-2. Persistent problems require levels 3-4.

**For persistent problems — three TOC questions** (T2.3):
- "What to change?" — root cause
- "What to change to?" — solution at the right level
- "How to cause the change?" — concrete steps

**Best used by:** whoever owns quality/consistency review. Senge's 12 system archetypes (T3.2) help recognize patterns at level 2.

## Descend Instruments (from abstract to concrete)

### Instrument 3: "How exactly?" (Victor's concretization)

Bret Victor's method: start with one concrete case → control parameters → only then generalize.

**Algorithm:**
1. "How exactly does this look in a *specific scene*?"
2. "What specifically does the character *do*? What do they see, hear, feel?"
3. "What is the *first physical action*?"

**When to apply:** procrastination, drowning in concepts, no next action.

**CLT mechanism:** concrete representations reduce procrastination. GTD's "next physical action" works by the same logic.

**Best used by:** whoever owns concrete execution / scene-level work.

### Instrument 4: Rubber Duck (forced externalization)

Explaining a problem aloud engages different brain areas and forces transition from internal "I know" to external "I formulate" (T7.1).

**Algorithm:**
1. Choose a "listener" (physical object, colleague, empty chair)
2. Explain the problem from scratch, as if the listener knows nothing about the context
3. Note moments where the explanation "jams" — those are gaps in understanding

**Key advantage:** works even during tunneling (P7), because physical action (speaking) is easier to initiate than internal reflection.

**Best used by:** anyone on the team.

### Instrument 5: Writing = Thinking

"The person who says he knows what he thinks but cannot express it usually does not know what he thinks" (Adler). Writing strips away disguises: poor thinking has nowhere to hide (T7.3).

**Algorithm:**
1. Write current understanding as connected prose (not bullet points — connected explanation)
2. Re-read as if it were someone else's text
3. Places where the text "falls apart" = places where understanding is shallow

**Best used by:** whoever owns output quality / prose.

## Horizontal Instruments (alternatives at same level)

### Instrument 6: Idea Quota

Direct antidote to Einstellung (T1.1) and premature closure (T1.6).

**Algorithm:**
1. Current option is banned from being repeated — only new ones
2. Minimum 3 alternatives before selection
3. MECE check (Minto, T2.5): is the set of alternatives complete and non-overlapping?
4. If <3 — continue generating

**4 Utley strategies** (Stanford d.school) when ideas are scarce:
1. Decide not to decide (defer)
2. Increase the number of ideas (more generation)
3. Explore the problem deeper (maybe it's wrongly framed)
4. Reframe the problem

**Best used by:** whoever facilitates the process. All participants generate.

### Instrument 7: Inversion (Munger / Black Hat)

"How can this scene/arc/decision FAIL?"

**Algorithm:**
1. Flip the question: not "how to do well?" → "how to guarantee failure?"
2. List all failure modes
3. Verify: is the current plan protected against each?
4. Second-order thinking: consequences of consequences

**Best used by:** whoever owns critical review / devil's advocate.

## Scale Switch Instruments (between system levels)

### Instrument 8: Narrative System Operator (TRIZ, T2.1)

3x3 matrix — the most formalized instrument for forced level switching. Direct Einstellung antidote: forced traversal of all 9 cells prevents fixation on one solution.

**Algorithm:**

|           | Past (backstory) | Present (current beat) | Future (consequences) |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **Subsystem** (scene, MRU) | What led to this scene? | What happens inside? | How will the scene change the next one? |
| **System** (chapter/arc) | Where did the arc start? | Where is the arc now? | Where does the arc lead? |
| **Supersystem** (book/series) | World context before events | How do events fit the whole? | Consequences for the series |

1. Fill all 9 cells
2. Empty cell = potential structural gap
3. Check consistency horizontally (past → present → future) and vertically (subsystem → system → supersystem)

**Best used by:** whoever owns structural analysis.

### Instrument 9: Leverage Points (Meadows, T3.1)

Not all world elements are equal for the story. Meadows' 12 points grouped for the book team:

1. **Paradigms** (highest lever) — why is the world structured this way? What is the base mental model of inhabitants?
2. **System goals** — why does the faction/institution exist? What are they trying to achieve?
3. **Rules** — world rules, norms, constraints
4. **Structure** — flows of resources, power, information, feedback loops
5. **Parameters** (lowest lever) — numbers, dates, sizes

Changing a paradigm cascades to everything below. Changing a parameter changes nothing above. Work at the highest available lever.

**Best used by:** whoever owns domain knowledge / worldbuilding.

## Timer Switch

### Instrument 10: 30-Minute Rule (T7.4)

Main compensator for the metacognition paradox (P7): the timer fires regardless of mental state.

**Algorithm:**
1. STOP. Note what has been done for the last 30 minutes
2. Identify current level (abstract ↑ / concrete ↓)
3. Switch to the OPPOSITE level for 15 minutes
4. Return and evaluate: did new understanding emerge?
5. If not — the problem is not the level but missing information → switch to research

**Best used by:** whoever facilitates the process.

## Six Socratic Questions (universal navigator, T7.2)

Cover ALL movement directions (up/down/horizontal/meta), unlike "5 Why?" (up only) or "How exactly?" (down only).

| Type | Direction | Example for book team |
|------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Clarification** | ↓ concrete | "What exactly do you mean by 'the arc doesn't work'?" |
| **Assumptions** | → deeper | "What is this assumption based on? Is there evidence or is it inherited?" |
| **Evidence** | ↓ to data | "Where in the existing material is this confirmed?" |
| **Perspectives** | ↔ horizontal | "How does this look from a different stakeholder's / audience's point of view?" |
| **Consequences** | ↑ to system | "If we take this path, what does it mean for the larger structure? For future work?" |
| **Meta-question** | ⤴ meta-level | "Is this the right question? What are we really trying to understand?" |

## Porpoising (T5.1) — Showrunner Practice

Both poles are pathological (Kanter, HBR):
- **Only zoom in** → loss of strategy, goal displacement
- **Only zoom out** → loss of context, castles in the air

Practice: dive into specifics (scene, line of dialogue) → surface to vision (theme, controlling idea) → dive again. The deepest insights are born **in the transitions** between levels, not at any single one (Victor, T5.2).
